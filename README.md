## Project Motivation

Earthquakes can cause severe damage to infrastructure and communities, making it essential to understand how key earthquake attributes relate to one another. Core parameters such as magnitude and depth are known to influence the severity and overall impact of seismic events. Prior research suggests that seismic activity does not occur randomly; rather, it often exhibits structured patterns and “memory,” where characteristics of earlier events influence subsequent earthquakes.

Understanding these relationships is critical for improving seismic hazard assessment and regional risk analysis.

---

## Research Objective

This study analyzes data from a recent seismic incident in Türkiye, a tectonically active region with frequent earthquake activity. The primary objective is to:

- Identify statistical relationships and dependencies among earthquake parameters  
- Examine magnitude–depth interactions and other key correlations  
- Evaluate whether identified seismic patterns may be transferable to the Pacific Northwest  

By comparing patterns across regions, this research aims to assess how geological settings influence earthquake behavior.

---

## Methodological Approach

Using the available seismic dataset, this study applies statistical modeling techniques to examine:

- Correlations among earthquake magnitude, depth, and other attributes  
- Potential dependency structures within seismic activity  
- Regional similarities and differences in parameter relationships  

The analysis emphasizes interpretability and statistical significance to ensure robust conclusions.

---

## Expected Contribution

The results will provide a comparative perspective on regional seismic behavior and highlight how interactions among earthquake attributes may vary across geological environments.

These insights contribute to:

- Improved seismic risk assessment  
- Enhanced regional hazard modeling  
- Better-informed urban planning and community preparedness strategies  

More broadly, this project demonstrates how data-driven statistical approaches can be applied to natural hazard research to make complex seismic patterns more interpretable and actionable.
